Options & the Iron Condor

Built for you, explained like you’ve never heard any of this before

Jeff — you don’t need a finance degree for any of this. You need the right pictures. That’s what these lessons are: everyday pictures for every idea. Read them slowly. Every block builds on the last one. By the end of this page, the words “Iron Condor” will make complete sense.

The Mindset · Start Here

Most people bet. Poppa runs the casino.

Here’s how most people think about trading: “I think this stock is going up, so I’ll buy it and hope.” That’s a bet. It’s basically gambling with extra steps.

Poppa does something completely different. He doesn’t bet on which way the stock moves. He collects money from people who are scared it will move.

The picture that ties everything together Think about a car insurance company. Every month you pay your premium. The insurance company collects it, hopes nothing happens to your car, and keeps the money most months because most months you don’t crash. Poppa IS the insurance company. He sells financial insurance to nervous investors. They pay him a premium. Most of the time, nothing dramatic happens, and he keeps the cash. That’s the whole strategy in one sentence.
Block 1 · The Foundation

What is an “option”? It’s a coupon with a deadline.

The pizza coupon A pizza shop hands you a coupon: “Pay me $2 right now, and any time in the next 30 days, you can buy this pizza for $10 — even if the price goes up to $15.” You paid $2 for the right to lock in that $10 price. That coupon is an option.
  • The premium — the $2 you paid. Always paid upfront.
  • The strike price — the $10 locked-in price.
  • The expiration — the 30-day deadline. After this date the coupon is worthless.
Jeff’s plain-English translation An option is a contract that gives someone the right to buy or sell something at a set price before a deadline. The buyer pays a fee (premium) for that right. The seller collects that fee.
Block 2 · Two Types

Calls and Puts — there are only two kinds of options.

  • A CALL option — the right to buy a stock. People buy calls when they think the price is going up.
  • A PUT option — the right to sell a stock. People buy puts when they think the price is going down.
Memory trick — never forget this CALL = price might Climb.    PUT = price might Plummet.
Why this matters to you You’re not buying calls or puts. Poppa SELLS them. But you need to know what they are before you understand why selling them makes money.
Block 3 · The Key Move

Poppa is on the other side — he SELLS the coupons.

For every person buying an option, someone has to sell it to them. That seller is Poppa. He collects their premium upfront. Right now. In his account. Immediately.

Then he waits. If nothing dramatic happens, the option expires worthless. Poppa keeps every dollar of that premium and does it all over again next month.

Back to the insurance company State Farm doesn’t pray your car crashes. They pray it doesn’t. Every month you pay, they keep it. Poppa’s model is identical — sell the insurance, collect the premium, hope nothing exciting happens.
Block 4 · Your Best Friend

Time is a melting ice cube — and Poppa loves it.

The ice cube The buyer paid for an ice cube. They need it to stay frozen (they need the stock to make a big move) or their cube melts into nothing. Poppa the seller is just watching the cube melt. Every drop of water = money in his pocket.
The word for this Traders call time decay “theta.” It’s the daily dollar value that melts off an option as time passes. Sellers love theta. Buyers hate it. Theta is your paycheck, delivered one day at a time.
Block 5 · The Strategy

The Iron Condor — drawing a box and getting paid to stay inside.

Poppa draws a box around a stock’s current price. He bets the stock stays inside the box until the deadline. He gets paid for that bet.

The highway lane picture Imagine a car driving on a highway. Poppa builds guardrails on both sides and makes this bet: “I don’t think this car is going to crash through the left OR the right guardrail before we reach the exit.” He collects money for that bet. If the car stays in the lane, he wins.
  • A put spread (left side of the box) — bets the stock won’t fall below his left guardrail.
  • A call spread (right side of the box) — bets the stock won’t climb above his right guardrail.

He collects TWO premiums. If the stock stays in the lane until expiration, both expire worthless. Boring is his best friend. Nothing happening = payday.

Why is it called an “Iron Condor”? A condor is a big bird with wings spread wide on both sides. The shape of this trade — with wings spreading left and right of the stock price — looks like that bird from above. Just picture the box.
Block 6 · The Safety Net

The guardrails — so one bad day can’t destroy everything.

On each side of the box, Poppa also buys a cheaper option further out. This is his airbag.

  • On the downside: he buys a put even further below his sold put. If the stock crashes through both, his bought put stops the bleeding.
  • On the upside: he buys a call even further above his sold call. Caps the damage if the stock shoots way up.
This is what “defined risk” means Your maximum possible loss is locked in and known before you ever click buy. You can still lose that max amount — but you’ll never lose MORE than that amount. The guardrails make sure of it.
Block 7 · The Paycheck

The 5–10% rent check — how Poppa measures if a trade is worth doing.

What ROC means in plain English ROC = Return on Capital. “How much am I collecting compared to how much I’m risking?” Poppa puts $465 at risk and collects $35 in premium → ROC = $35 ÷ $465 = 7.5%.
  • Below 5% = doesn’t pay enough. Skip it.
  • 5% to 10% = the sweet spot. Good rent for the risk taken.
  • Above 10% = suspiciously high. The market is usually paying extra because something scary is about to happen.
The whole thing in one sentence

Poppa is an insurance company who sells melting ice cubes on both sides of a calm stock, draws a box it probably won’t leave, keeps guardrails so a disaster can’t wipe him out, and collects 5–10% rent for betting nothing exciting happens.

Part 2 · A Real Trade · Step by Step

Watch one condor get built, then win — and lose.

Pretend stock: “Calm Co.” trading at $50. Poppa thinks it’s staying boring for 30 days.

$60 BUY Call ← top guardrail (airbag if stock rockets) $55 SELL Call ┌ │ THE BOX — stock should stay in here $50 • now • │ Calm Co. is right here today │ $45 SELL Put └ $40 BUY Put ← bottom guardrail (airbag if stock crashes)

Collects $35 premium upfront. Brokerage holds $465 deposit (worst-case loss). ROC: 7.5% — sweet spot. Then he waits.

✓ The win — stock stays in the lane

30 days later, Calm Co. closes at $51. Inside the box. All four options expired worthless. Poppa keeps the full $35. Does it again next month.

✗ The loss — stock crashes out

Bad news hits. Calm Co. drops to $42. The $40 guardrail kicks in and caps the damage. Loss stops at exactly $465 — the number he knew before placing the trade.

Jeff — here’s what this looks like in real life Small wins, often. Occasionally a loss — but it’s always a known, controlled loss. String enough small wins together, and one capped loss doesn’t erase them. Picking the right stocks and the right boxes is the whole game — and that’s what the screener does for Poppa.
Part 3 · The Risks

What breaks this strategy — and how to avoid it.

  • Big sudden moves — if the stock shoots or crashes hard enough to blow through the box, Poppa takes the loss. This is the nature of the trade.
  • Earnings announcements — stocks can jump 10–20% in hours. Poppa never holds a condor into earnings. Hard rule, no exceptions.
  • Market-wide fear spikes — when the whole market panics, sold options get more expensive to buy back even if the stock hasn’t moved much. The ice cube temporarily re-freezes.
  • Slow drift toward the edge — the stock doesn’t crash; it quietly creeps toward one rail over weeks. Know when to close early rather than hoping it reverses.
  • Too tight a box — a narrow lane pays more rent but the car leaves it more often. Wider = safer.
Reality Check
⚠ Read this twice, Jeff

“Defined-risk” does not mean “safe.”

Defined-risk means: your worst case is known and capped. It does NOT mean the strategy is safe or low-risk.

Your wins are small ($35). Your max loss is bigger ($465). You survive by winning more often than you lose. One steamroller can erase months of premiums in a single day.

What actually keeps this working Discipline: size positions small, spread across many stocks, dodge earnings every time, take profits early, cut losses before max. The screener enforces those rules automatically.
Part 4 · The Tool

What the screener actually does — and why it matters.

Out of hundreds of stocks, only a small handful have a box worth selling on any given day. The screener is the bird dog. It walks the whole field automatically, every day, and points only at trades that pass all of Poppa’s rules.

  • Right rent — ROC 5–10%. Not too thin, not suspiciously high.
  • Wide enough lane — both sides have 85%+ probability of the stock staying inside.
  • Insurance is overpriced — the market is paying more for fear than the actual odds justify. That overpayment is the real edge.
  • No earnings in the window — the demolition derby is nowhere near the deadline.
